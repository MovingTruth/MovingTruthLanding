What if dying is less like an ending and more like changing rooms?

Not a promise. Not a belief system. Just a question worth staying with long enough to feel its weight.

Because here is what we actually know about death.

We know what it looks like from the outside.

We have no reliable report from the inside.

Every civilisation in human history has offered an answer — heaven and hell, its older and darker opposite, reincarnation, ancestors, spirits, the void, the next world, the dream — and of all of them, hell is the one most traditions were very careful to describe in detail.

That is worth noticing.

Not because the traditions are right. But because the refusal is so universal.

What if that refusal is not wishful thinking?

What if it is pattern recognition — something the human animal has always sensed but never quite been able to say clearly?

Consider this.

What if you've already died before, and you just don't remember starting over?

The you that is reading this has no memory of before you were born. None. That gap — that clean, total absence of experience — is something you have already survived once. You arrived here from nowhere that you can name, already formed, already you.

What if that is not as unusual as it sounds?

What if the life you are living right now is someone else's afterlife?

What if you pick up exactly where you left off — just not here?

What if death is simply the part where you stop being able to tell anyone what you see?

None of these are answers.

They are not meant to be.

They are meant to disturb the certainty a little. To make the familiar assumption — that death is a full stop — feel slightly less inevitable than it did this morning.

Because the one thing we know for certain is that nobody has lived without dying.

And the one thing we have never been able to prove is what that means.
