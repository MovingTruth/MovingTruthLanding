When did you last have thirty uninterrupted minutes to think?

Not to scroll. Not to work. Not to manage the list. To just — think. Follow one thought all the way to where it leads.

Try to remember.

---

Your attention is the most valuable thing you have. More valuable than your time. More valuable than your money. Because it is the thing that decides where both of those go.

And it is being harvested.

Every notification is a hand on your shoulder. Every infinite scroll is a path with no end. Every autoplay is a door that opens before you decided to open it. None of this is accidental. There are rooms full of engineers whose entire job is to calculate how to keep your eyes on a screen for one more minute. One more minute, multiplied by a billion people, is the business model.

Your attention is not what you use to consume the internet.

Your attention is the product.

---

Think about the to-do list.

Not the one you wrote this morning. The one that has been following you for months. The one that grows faster than it shrinks. The one where finishing three things reveals four more underneath.

Now think about why.

Every tool designed to help you manage your time requires your attention to use it. Every app. Every notification. Every system that promises to make you more efficient requires you to be in it, checking it, updating it. The tools for managing distraction are themselves a distraction.

Everything became a distraction from the last thing.

That is not a personal failure of organisation. That is the texture of a world designed to fragment your attention into pieces too small to do anything with.

A person with thirty uninterrupted minutes can ask a dangerous question.

A person managing seventeen half-finished tasks cannot.

---

Piece one asked what happened to your money and your time.

Piece two is the answer to the second part.

Your time went here. Into the fragment. Into the list that grows. Into the hand on your shoulder that arrived before you decided you wanted company.

Nobody asked if you wanted to live this way.

The question is whether that was an oversight.
